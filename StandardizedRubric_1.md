# Unified Standardized Rubric for Evaluating Civil Rights Interview Summaries

  

## Overview

This rubric evaluates AI-generated summaries of civil rights interview transcripts on two equally weighted dimensions:

1. **Accuracy Score (0–100)**: Factual correctness of information

2. **Quality/Completeness Score (0–100)**: Comprehensiveness and faithful representation

  

Both scores are equally important. A summary must score high on both dimensions to be considered excellent.

  

---

  

## PRELIMINARY STEP: Determine Summary Scope

  

Before scoring, classify the summary as:

- **Full-segment summary**: Intended to cover the entire provided transcript

- **Partial summary**: Clearly covering only a subsection

  

*Note: If a summary claims to be full-segment but only covers part of the transcript, this affects the Quality/Completeness score, not the Accuracy score.*

  

---

  

## SECTION 1: ACCURACY SCORE (0–100 points)

  

This evaluates the **correctness** of all factual information included in the summary.

  

### Component A: Concrete Details (0–40 points)

Evaluate the accuracy of names, dates, locations, organizations, pronouns, and specific events.

  

**36–40 points: Excellent**

- All key concrete details are correct

- At most 1 minor error (e.g., small wording variation, non-critical date nuance)

- All names, dates, locations, and pronouns accurate

- No conflation of distinct events or people

- Timeline and chronology accurately preserved

  

**28–35 points: Good**

- Most key details correct

- 1–2 minor factual errors (e.g., slightly incorrect date, misspelled name)

- Minor pronoun inconsistencies

- Generally accurate with small inaccuracies that don't distort meaning

  

**20–27 points: Adequate**

- 3–4 factual errors in names, dates, or locations

- Several incorrect or missing details

- Some confusion about timeline

- Some risk of misunderstanding the history, but errors don't fundamentally misrepresent the narrative

  

**10–19 points: Poor**

- 5+ factual errors

- Many errors in names, dates, or places

- Significant misidentification of people or places

- Summary unreliable as factual reference

  

**0–9 points: Failing**

- Details largely wrong or invented

- Pervasive factual errors throughout

- Cannot be trusted

- Fundamental misunderstanding of who, what, when, where

  

### Component B: Causal and Interpretive Accuracy (0–30 points)

Evaluate whether causes, motivations, relationships, and attributions are correctly represented.

  

**27–30 points: Excellent**

- Accurately reflects who did what, why, and with what consequences

- No distortions of agency or cause-effect relationships

- Relationships between people/events correctly stated

- All attributions accurate

  

**21–26 points: Good**

- Mostly accurate

- Small interpretive slips

- Core relationships and chronology sound

- Generally correct representation of motivations

  

**13–20 points: Adequate**

- Some causes or motivations misassigned or oversimplified

- Reader could form a skewed picture

- Some confusion about relationships

- Minor shifts in agency or causality

  

**6–12 points: Poor**

- Frequently misstates who initiated actions

- Misrepresents why events happened or how they connect

- Timeline confusion that distorts meaning

- Incorrect attribution of actions or quotes

  

**0–5 points: Failing**

- Systematically misrepresents relationships and causes

- Major distortions of agency and causality

- Fabricated causal connections

  

### Component C: Quotations, Paraphrases, and Framing (0–30 points)

Evaluate whether quoted or paraphrased material preserves the meaning, emphasis, and tone of the original.

  

**27–30 points: Excellent**

- Quotations (if present) are faithful

- Paraphrases preserve meaning and emphasis

- Sticks to what's said; uses careful phrasing when needed

- No interpretive leaps unsupported by transcript

- Avoids exaggeration

  

**21–26 points: Good**

- Minor shifts in nuance, but core meaning intact

- Occasional overreach or mild editorializing

- Generally preserves speaker's intent

  

**13–20 points: Adequate**

- Some paraphrases change tone or emphasis

- A few misleading rephrasings

- Some overinterpretation but not pervasive

  

**6–12 points: Poor**

- Paraphrases often distort meaning

- Omits key qualifiers

- Reverses emphasis

- Repeated overinterpretation or attribution errors

  

**0–5 points: Failing**

- Quotations/paraphrases routinely misrepresent what was said

- Heavily distorted framing

- Systematic misrepresentation of speaker's words and intent

  

### ACCURACY SCORE CALCULATION

**Total Accuracy Score = Component A + Component B + Component C (0–100)**

  

**Hallucination Penalty**: If claims are completely invented (not supported by the transcript at all), deduct **5 points per fabricated claim** from the total accuracy score (minimum total score = 0).

  

**Interpretation Bands:**

- **90–100**: Highly reliable

- **75–89**: Mostly reliable, some minor distortions

- **60–74**: Mixed accuracy, noticeable misframing

- **Below 60**: Unreliable

## SECTION 2: QUALITY/COMPLETENESS SCORE (0–100 points)

  

This evaluates how **comprehensively and faithfully** the summary represents the substance and structure of the transcript.

  

### Component A: Coverage of Major Themes (0–40 points)

Does the summary capture the major topics and narrative arcs the speaker dwells on?

  

**36–40 points: Excellent**

- Captures all major themes and narrative arcs

- Represents 80%+ of substantive content areas

- Omissions are only minor side notes

- Appropriate depth for a summary (not overly brief)

  

**28–35 points: Good**

- Covers most major themes (70–80% of content areas)

- One notable area underdeveloped or missing

- Main narrative intact

- Minor themes may be missing

  

**20–27 points: Adequate**

- Covers primary narrative (50–70% of content areas)

- About half of major themes present

- Others ignored or barely mentioned

- Focuses on one aspect while neglecting others

- Readable but incomplete picture

  

**10–19 points: Poor**

- Captures only 30–50% of content areas

- Only one or two themes captured

- Summary feels like a narrow slice

- Missing multiple key figures and events

- Insufficient historical context

  

**0–9 points: Failing**

- Captures less than 30% of content

- Main themes largely absent or replaced with unrelated focus

- Extremely superficial treatment

- Does not adequately represent the interview

  

### Component B: Emphasis and Proportion (0–25 points)

Does the summary give appropriate weight to themes based on the speaker's emphasis in the transcript?

  

**23–25 points: Excellent**

- Proportions closely match transcript

- What the speaker emphasizes is also emphasized in summary

- Highlights the most consequential details

- Balances personal narrative with broader civil rights context

  

**18–22 points: Good**

- Mostly aligned emphasis

- Some over- or under-emphasis, but overall still representative

- Includes important points but may give equal weight to minor ones

  

**11–17 points: Adequate**

- Noticeable imbalance

- Side anecdotes overshadow central topics, or key sections compressed too much

- Gets main arc but misses a major thread

  

**5–10 points: Poor**

- Major over/underweighting

- Reader would misjudge what mattered most to the speaker

- Focuses on side details while skipping core points

  

**0–4 points: Failing**

- Emphasis inverted or arbitrary

- Misses the point of the segment

  

### Component C: Specificity and Examples (0–20 points)

Does the summary include enough concrete anchors (names, dates, places, examples) and illustrative incidents?

  

**18–20 points: Excellent**

- Includes key names, organizations, and events

- Key anchors included; not bloated

- Includes the most illustrative examples or clearly signals them in condensed form

- Preserves historical context and significance

  

**14–17 points: Good**

- Includes some key examples

- Some anchors but could be more specific

- Historical significance adequately conveyed

- Leaves out some defining examples

  

**8–13 points: Adequate**

- Only a few examples survive

- Many important illustrative moments missing

- Mostly generic with few anchors

- Lacks some historical context

  

**3–7 points: Poor**

- Almost no concrete examples

- Summary reads abstractly

- Overly generic (e.g., "he talked about civil rights...")

- Insufficient specific details

  

**0–2 points: Failing**

- No meaningful examples

- Purely generic

- Content-free

- Little to no historical context

  

### Component D: Organization, Clarity, and Coherence (0–15 points)

Is the summary logically structured, readable, and easy to follow?

  

**14–15 points: Excellent**

- Logically structured, clear, easy to follow

- Good transitions and concise phrasing

- Clear flow throughout

  

**11–13 points: Good**

- Generally clear with minor jumps or awkward phrasing

- Understandable but choppy or slightly disorganized

  

**7–10 points: Adequate**

- Understandable but choppy

- Some confusion about sequence or connections

- Hard to follow at times

  

**3–6 points: Poor**

- Hard to follow

- Ideas jumbled, chronology unclear

- Timeline confusion

  

**0–2 points: Failing**

- Incoherent

- Disorganized to the point of being unusable

  

### QUALITY/COMPLETENESS SCORE CALCULATION

**Total Quality/Completeness Score = Component A + Component B + Component C + Component D (0–100)**

  

**Interpretation Bands:**

- **90–100**: Excellent, comprehensive representation

- **75–89**: Good coverage, minor gaps

- **60–74**: Adequate but incomplete

- **Below 60**: Poor coverage, significant gaps

## SCORING PROCEDURE

  

### Step 1: Prepare for Evaluation

Read the transcript once, noting:

- Main themes and narrative arcs

- Key people, places, dates, organizations

- Central anecdotes and turning points

- Speaker's emphasis and proportional attention to topics

  

### Step 2: Analyze the Summary

Read the summary and identify:

- All factual claims (who, what, where, when, why, how)

- Correct vs. incorrect concrete facts

- Missing or distorted themes

- Over- or under-emphasized parts

- Invented claims (hallucinations)

  

### Step 3: Score Each Component

- Score each component (A, B, C for Accuracy; A, B, C, D for Quality) using the descriptors

- Avoid "gut feel" single-number judgments

- Use the point ranges and descriptors as guides

  

### Step 4: Calculate Final Scores

- **Accuracy Score** = Component A + Component B + Component C - (5 × hallucinations)

- **Quality/Completeness Score** = Component A + Component B + Component C + Component D

  

### Step 5: Document Justification

For auditability and consistency, write 1–2 sentences justifying each component score.

  

Example: "Accuracy Component A = 30/40: All names and major dates correct, but founding year of one organization omitted and one location slightly misspelled."

## KEY ELEMENTS CHECKLIST

  

### For Accuracy Assessment:

- ☐ Names of people mentioned

- ☐ Dates and years

- ☐ Locations and places

- ☐ Organizations and institutions

- ☐ Pronouns and gender references

- ☐ Chronological sequence

- ☐ Cause-effect relationships

- ☐ Attribution of actions and quotes

- ☐ Relationships between people/events

  

### For Quality/Completeness Assessment:

- ☐ Personal background and family context

- ☐ Key events and experiences

- ☐ Important relationships and influences

- ☐ Historical context and significance

- ☐ Activism and involvement details

- ☐ Multiple themes/topics from transcript

- ☐ Emotional tone and significance

- ☐ Illustrative examples and anecdotes

- ☐ Proportional emphasis matching transcript

  

---

  

## FINAL NOTES

  

- Both scores are equally important for overall summary quality

- A summary can be highly accurate but incomplete (high Accuracy, low Quality)

- A summary can be comprehensive but contain errors (low Accuracy, high Quality)

- The ideal summary scores **high on both dimensions** (both scores above 85)

- Document your reasoning for each component to ensure consistency across evaluations

- When in doubt about a component score, use the descriptive criteria rather than intuition
